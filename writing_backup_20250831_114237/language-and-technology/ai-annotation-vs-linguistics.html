Overview

TLDR

  • Linguistics gives the theory and categories.
  • Annotation turns theory into consistent labels.
  • Quality comes from clear guidelines and examples.
  • Small pilots first, then scale with reviews.

I start from language. I move to categories. I test those categories with data. I refine. I then create practical instructions so other annotators can use them and stay aligned with the intent.

Who this is for

Teams that want reliable datasets for model training. Startups building NLP features. Research groups standardising complex phenomena.

What you will learn

  • How to move from research notes to label sets
  • How to write an annotation guide
  • How to run calibration rounds

Step by step

  1. Define aims in one sentence.
  2. List phenomena and give plain examples.
  3. Draft labels and decision rules.
  4. Pilot 50 items with two annotators.
  5. Measure agreement. Revise.
  6. Lock v1.0 of the guide. Scale.

Examples

I include boundary cases. I write short rationales. I show both positive and negative instances. I keep language simple.

Pitfalls

  • Too many labels
  • Vague definitions
  • No edge cases
  • No review loop

Conclusion

When theory and practice stay close, annotation becomes easier. Models improve because the labels mean the same thing to everyone.
